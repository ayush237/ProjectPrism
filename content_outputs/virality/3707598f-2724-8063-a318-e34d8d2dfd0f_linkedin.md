Your team doesn't need a microservices architecture. It needs a well-structured monolith and a Postgres database with proper indexes.

Every quarter, I review design docs proposing we break down a perfectly functional CRUD application into a dozen distributed services. The justification is always "scalability." But 99% of the time, the only thing that actually scales is the latency, the CI/CD pipeline complexity, and the AWS bill.

When you split a monolith prematurely, you aren't solving engineering problems. You are just trading local method calls for network boundaries, eventual consistency, and distributed tracing nightmares. 

If your engineers are spending more time configuring Kubernetes ingress controllers than shipping business logic, you have over-engineered the solution. 

Good architecture isn't about implementing the most complex patterns you read about on a tech blog. It’s about choosing the simplest pattern that satisfies the current requirements while leaving a door open for future changes. 

Boring technology delivers software. Complex architecture delivers conference talks.
