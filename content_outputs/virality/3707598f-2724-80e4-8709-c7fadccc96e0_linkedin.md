**"Let's rewrite it from scratch."**

The six most dangerous words in software engineering.

Every time a team inherits a legacy system, the immediate instinct is to burn it down. The code is messy. The framework is outdated. The architecture seems completely irrational. 

But here is the reality of the "big rewrite" that you only learn after failing at it:

• **You are deleting context.** That "spaghetti code" you want to scrap is actually years of patched edge cases and undocumented business logic.
• **You will underestimate the scope.** Feature parity always takes at least 3x longer than the most conservative estimate.
• **You stall the business.** Product velocity drops to zero while engineering spends 8 months rebuilding what already exists.

Senior engineering isn't about spinning up greenfield microservices in the newest language. It's about understanding Chesterton’s Fence: Don’t tear down a wall until you understand exactly why it was built in the first place.

If the legacy system genuinely cannot scale, you don't drop a bomb on it. You use the Strangler Fig pattern. You carve out domains, route traffic incrementally, and deprecate the old system piece by piece while keeping the lights on.

Boring, incremental refactoring ships reliable systems. 
Big bang rewrites ship resumes.
